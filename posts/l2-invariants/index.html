Introduction

This semester (WiSe 2023/24) I took a course about \(\ell^2\)-Invariants1 at the University of Düsseldorf. As a reminder, \(\ell^2\) is the Hibert space of square-summable sequences of complex numbers. Meaning, a sequence \((a_n\in\mathbb{C})_{n=0}^\infty\) is in \(\ell^2\) if and only if \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty |a_n|^2 < \infty\). By defining an inner product on \(\ell^2\) as \(\langle a, b \rangle = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n \overline{b_n}\), we make \(\ell^2\) to a complete complex inner product space, i.e. a Hilbert space.

For context, this course was part of my master’s degree in mathematics and it was the culmination of a series of courses about Algebraic Topology, which I attended for the past two and a half years. Previous courses concentrated primarily on the basics of algebraic topology, such as homology and cohomology, and their applications to the classification of manifolds and the computation of homotopy groups and cohomology rings.

The tools in the form of algebraic structures and theorems that we learned in these coureses work well with finite CW-complexes and their finite coverings, after all we are just dealing with finitely generated modules in this case and concepts like determinants and betti numbers are well defined. However, when we want to study infinite CW-complexes, we need to use more sophisticated tools, such as \(\ell^2\)-Invariants, to understand the topology of these spaces. We can easily construct many infinite CW-complexes by considering infinite coverings of finite CW-complexes, and we can use \(\ell^2\)-Invariants to study the topology of these infinite coverings, as \(\ell^2\)-Invariants intrinsically cope better with infinite structures.
